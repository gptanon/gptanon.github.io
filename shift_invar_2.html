How does TT control the number of orders if it doesn't affect UU?

If "number of orders" refers to what's required to fully capture the input, then indeed the lowpass filter only captures existing energies within UUs, while energy propagation from UiUi to Ui+1Ui+1 is controlled only by wavelets.

A part of the answer, of course, is that lesser TT preserves more of UU's energy for all UU, so for any threshold th=∥x∥2−∥Sx∥2th=‖x‖2−‖Sx‖2 on what qualifies as "fully capture", TT will significantly affect the number of orders. And it's what we actually care about in practice. But if "fully" means the threshold is zero, things change: it doesn't matter what portion of energy the lowpass captures at each UU: if UU has any energy at all, then so does SS, and the only stopping criterion becomes U=0U=0, and that is independent of lowpassing.

The answer is, TT does affect UU. The standard formulation of scattering uses tight frames at each order, meaning wavelets cannot be designed independent of the lowpass. Lower TT lowpass occupies greater bandwidth in frequency, leaving less for wavelets to tile. So, while lower TT captures more of each UU's energy, each UU has less energy - so for sake of full capture, T=∞T=∞ is actually ideal.

This point isn't special to the zero threshold. Any very small threshold will do, so there's some crossover point at which lower TT is a detriment rather than improvement. An exception for all thresholds is T=0T=0, which terminates at zeroth order and returns Sx=xSx=x: zero is also a case of infinity that breaks things. What is special to the zero threshold, however, is that it does appear to be the case, though I'm not certain, that for nearly all inputs, the number of required orders is infinite (see "Addendum: errata"; this section isn't erroneous, rather the errata has explanations).

Subsampling is discrete-only, so how's it explain continuous scattering?

Indeed scattering is a continuous-time transform, while subsampling and its induced loss of information are discrete-only phenomena. Yet all statements hold.

The generalization is bandwidth reduction. "Temporal variability invariance" in continuous time is measured exactly the same, via a set cutoff on bandwidth that makes scattering coefficients "effectively bandlimited". Bandwidth is a strong measure of information capacity, so "redistributing variability" identically holds, as we limit the variability any one coefficient can encode. Subsampling provides an excellent interpretation of information capacity, which was described earlier. Link to bandwidth is also quantified in another way in formal information theory.

It's what motivates "scale of information bandwidth" - the "scale" is in frequency domain.

Can any lowpass filter work?

No.

Every lowpass has a TT per our definition, since every lowpass has a frequency cutoff and captures DC, so any lowpass enables "redistributing variability". But there's more to scattering. To illustrate, I'll use the "ideal" lowpass filter, among others:

  1. Sinc has enormous temporal expansiveness. It drastically worsens the loss of information due to unpadding; an input on order of seconds or minutes now spans years in effective support. Equivalently, awful time resolution.
  2. Sinc has negatives, no longer guaranteeing a strictly positive output for a strictly non-negative input (via modulus). This breaks the modulatory (e.g. AM) interpretation of scattering, forbids direct log norm, among other things.
  3. Sinc forbids tight frames, since there's no wavelets with sinc as scaling function, and workarounds would yield awful wavelets.
  4. Realized sinc, unless very long, has poor frequency resolution on top of awful time resolution, poor transition band behavior, and is of course significantly aliased (hence aliasing the redistributed variability).
  5. Sinc lacks O((1+|x|)−d−2)O((1+|x|)−d−2) decay (dd is dimension, 1 for 1D), violating a premise of warp-stability (Lemma E.1, Graph Invariant Scattering). This is a precise restatement of (1).

Some other criteria apply but most (if not all) lowpass filters already satisfy them (e.g. zero phase, real-valued). An important one is (6) smoothness, for stability, which is partly or wholly covered by 1-5 but helps to make it explicit.

For theory, each is a dealbreaker, but in practice that's only so for worst-case inputs (what demands lots of orders). Windowed sinc resolves (1) and (4) and makes it perfectly acceptable in practice, and it's what I've done for JTFS's frequential averaging for its superior main lobe and far less blurring; negatives are in minority and are handled, and (3) is negligible for two orders.

Vs Cont-Net Invariants?

It's fundamentally different: CNN discards irrelevant variability, scattering redistributes variability. This is clear from the compute graph:

  • The standard CNN progressively downsamples, in series, so the input to the network cannot possibly be recovered from what's fed to the classifier.
  • Scattering feeds the outputs of its entire compute pipeline, not just the final order. Its outputs are also complementary in information by design, which is impossible with in-series downsampling.

Of course there's also up-sampled or invertible CNNs, but that discussion's more complicated. A key distinction is such nets have generative elements, while scattering is only a decomposition.

"But filters are infinite in support", so how can there be a "TT"?

The goal isn't 100% correct transform, it's 100% correct output.

The change in output due to a 0.1% change in features is 0% for a good classifier, and negligible in general. If it's not negligible, that's a classifier problem: the error due to real-world imprecision (noise, sampling, etc) is anything but 0%, and is almost always more than 0.1%. The error that's imposed by defining TT via a cutoff is, practical worst case, 2%, and typically 0.1%. That's not just throwing numbers around, it's how I designed and tested my implementation (!= Kymatio's).

Note, "classifier" above can be replaced with anything else that the features are fed to.

Further, "100% correct" is bad news. Let's take 99.999% since literally 100% is largely impossible. Since it means much lower subsample-ability, the features would be extremely correlated: the information density, or amount of unique variability encoded per sample, would be very low. It can even make sense to set 10% loss to reduce overfitting.

Why not lower TT instead of using more orders?

If greater TT causes the need for higher orders to recover lost information, why not just lower TT and get something "between" UU and SS?

Because then we're not just redistributing variability, we're also duplicating it. If we used T=16T=16 earlier, we'd leave some oscillations in U1U1, yet those oscillations are completely described in U2U2 and hence are redundant. This is formalized with energy analysis: T<2JT<2J cannot keep the LP sum below 11 and breaks contractiveness.

Another perspective is that lower TT is just making sc(x) approach x; we're undoing our work.

Greater TT is also greater shift-insensitivity (discussed later), often beneficial. And, it entails a greater operational "size of deformation" in warp stability, i.e. "linearization" is in effect for greater deformations (but the equation's upper bound remains applicable for any TT).

However, lower TT is still valid and useful feature engineering. Separate topic but in a nutshell, in practice we won't have enough orders, or having enough isn't worth it - whether due to compute expense or the added output size not being "worth the attention" of a classifier.
